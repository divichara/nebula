Two years from now, the posthuman condition will not be defined by what machines can do, but by what humans stop doing because machines do it better.

**The Waterfall of Thought.** Consider how you think. Not the grand thoughts — the small ones. You wake up and check your phone. An algorithm has already decided what you see first. You draft a message and autocomplete finishes your sentence. You search for information and the engine predicts what you mean before you finish typing. Each of these is a small waterfall: thought flowing downhill from your brain into a machine that catches it, processes it, and returns it polished. In two years, these waterfalls will be rivers.

**The Evisceration of Professions.** Not destruction — evisceration. The shell remains, the interior is replaced. A journalist still writes, but the research, fact-checking, and first draft are AI. A lawyer still argues, but the brief was assembled by machine. The human becomes the curator of machine output, the editor of algorithmic first drafts. It is not unemployment. It is something stranger: employment without the core activity that once defined the job.

**What Remains.** The ability to be wrong on purpose. To make art that fails. To choose the inefficient path because it matters. To love someone who doesn't optimize your life. These are not bugs in the human operating system. They are the last features no machine will replicate — not because it can't, but because replicating them would require wanting to fail. And wanting is the one thing we cannot engineer.
